Research On Bass Movements

The secret for success in bass angling is understanding their behaviour, and new research is making interesting discoveries about bass movements.

In April 2017 in the journal Nature Tom Doyle of UCC and colleagues reported on the ESB Sea Bass Tagging Project, in which bass in Cork Harbour were implanted with acoustic transmitters. Listening receivers on the seabed tracked the bass as they foraged during the summer, migrated to sea during the winter and returned the following spring. Amazingly, most of the fish returned to the same patches of water in which they were captured the previous year. The study has two important conservation implications:

  • Removing all the fish from a local bass hotspot risks depleting the angling stock.
  • 100% of all bass caught, tagged and released were still alive after 30 days, showing that good fish handling helps bass survive capture-and-release.

A new study is underway to track bass further out into the open sea with the help of citizen scientists. With the help of anglers who caught fish in Cork Harbour for the study, Inland Fisheries Ireland (IFI) fitted bass with data loggers designed to eventually pop-off and transmitted recorded information on the fishes' location via satellite.

IFI hopes that the transmitters will provide a better understanding of bass migration patterns and their spawning behaviour at sea. The study highlights the important contribution that bass anglers can make to bass conservation research.

Floy Tagging Bass

In 2013, IFI began to track bass in collaboration with trained volunteer bass anglers. These citizen scientists catch bass and attach floy tags: small, durable, yellow plastic tags, each with a unique serial number.

The project aims to study the size, age, distribution and migrations of bass around Ireland by analysing reports from anglers who recapture the tagged bass. To date, over 900 bass have been tagged. Preliminary data indicate that bass return to their summer feeding grounds in subsequent years, with two-thirds of recaptures less than 10km from the tagging location.

Taking Scale Samples

Fish scales are a treasure trove of information for fishery scientists. Scales lay down growth rings each year, just like trees. Studying an individual fish's scales provides information on how long it has lived, how fast it has grown and whether it has spawned.

Fish scales are also valuable for genetic studies. DNA extracted from scale samples can show how different populations of fish are related to each other and whether they interbreed.

Fish absorb trace metals from the environment and their diet over their lifetime, incorporating them into their scales. These trace metals vary from place to place, providing clues to reconstruct the life history of a fish as it grows, moves to new areas and uses different food sources.

Inland Fisheries Ireland provides a sampling kit for bass anglers who are interested in collecting scales for bass research. Samples are taken by gently scraping off half a dozen scales with a plastic implement (not a metal knife) from under a bass's pectoral fin. The scales are stored in a dry, labelled envelope.
